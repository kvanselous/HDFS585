Findings

Drake, Aos, & Miller (2009) complied these results into five categories, separating the results by adult programs, juvenile programs, prevention programs, adult programs requiring additional research, and juvenile programs requiring additional research. Largely, all the programs included for prevention of crime reduction showed a significant decrease in crime and return of tax payer dollars. These programs included pre-k education for low-income 3â€“4-year-old children and nurse family partnerships for mothers and children. Programs for adults that decreased crime and costs to tax payers included vocational and general education programs in prison, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy programs in prisons and communities, drug treatment in prison such as therapeutic communities and outpatient services, drug treatment in community, and adult drug courts. Programs for juvenile's that decreased crime and costs to tax payers included programs with Functional Family Therapy, Teen courts, restorative justice programs for low-risk offenders, and family integrated transition programs. Programs that did not show a decrease in crime and increased costs for tax payers included Intensive Supervision programs for adults and juveniles. One program for youth that increased crime and increased cost to tax payers included the Scared Straight program.
